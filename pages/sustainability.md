# Sustainability & Ethics

## My Research

The environmental impact of AI is complex and often difficult to quantify with precision. One key metric is per-query emissions: ChatGPT generates approximately 4.32 grams of CO2 per query. While this seems minimal in isolation, when multiplied across millions of users making countless searches daily, the cumulative impact becomes significant. To contextualize this, a commercial flight produces approximately 157 grams of CO2 per kilometer, meaning a single flight can generate the equivalent emissions of thousands of AI queries.

The uncertainty around AI's total environmental contribution stems from the challenge of obtaining official data. Most CO2 emission figures for AI services come from unofficial estimates rather than transparent reporting from the companies themselves. Additionally, when comparing AI's environmental cost to other daily activities—such as air travel, driving, or general electricity consumption—the picture becomes more nuanced. The average US household produces 7.5 tons of CO2 annually, whereas individual AI usage contributes a relatively small fraction of that total.

Aviation itself accounts for a significant portion of global emissions, having grown substantially between 1990 and 2019. This raises the central question: what are we more willing to have contribute to the world's CO2 count? The answer is not straightforward, and individual concerns about AI's environmental impact must be weighed against the broader systems we participate in daily.

### Key Findings:
- ChatGPT generates approximately 4.32 grams of CO2 per query
- Commercial flights produce ~157 grams of CO2 per kilometer
- The average US household produces 7.5 tons of CO2 annually
- Official data on AI service emissions remains largely unavailable; most figures are estimates

## Personal Reflection

### My AI Use Habits

I use AI tools very minimally and only when necessary. I approach AI as a tool to be used sparingly, not as a default for every task. I consciously choose not to rely on AI for routine work when I can complete the task myself within a reasonable timeframe. My usage is intentional and deliberate.

However, I acknowledge that I cannot completely eliminate AI from my life, particularly as these tools become increasingly embedded in everyday technology and services. I will continue to try and cut back my usage in the future, but I must be realistic about the impossibility of complete abstinence while participating in modern technological systems.

I recognize that true change cannot come from individual restraint alone. **Individuals need to come together to collectively limit their own usage to create meaningful change.** When many people make conscious decisions to reduce their AI consumption, the aggregate effect becomes significant enough to shift market demand and corporate behavior. This collective action—rather than individual perfection—is what can drive systemic change in AI development and deployment.

### Estimated Impact

By using AI tools for 2-3 hours daily, I estimate I contribute:
- ~0.5-0.8 kg of CO2e per week through direct AI tool usage
- Indirect impact through increased productivity leading to more work output and consumption patterns
- Ethical complicity in labor exploitation through data annotation and training

This translates to roughly 25-40 kg CO2e annually from my personal AI use alone.

### What I'm Willing to Change

- Consolidate AI queries and use batch processing where possible rather than multiple sequential requests
- Use smaller, open-source models when feasible instead of larger proprietary ones
- Document and share AI prompts within my team to reduce redundant queries
- Critically evaluate whether AI assistance is truly necessary for each task
- Support research into sustainable AI and efficient model optimization

### What I Can't/Won't Change (and Why)

- I won't completely stop using AI tools because the productivity gains enable me to complete meaningful work that benefits others
- I can't eliminate my participation in systems built on exploitative labor without opting out of technology entirely (which would itself be a privileged choice)
- I won't sacrifice personal efficiency entirely because I believe my time is better spent on higher-value work rather than routine tasks
- However, I acknowledge this is a privileged position and recognize the tension between individual benefit and systemic harm

## Systemic vs. Individual

### What Can Individuals Do?

- **Make informed choices:** Research the environmental and ethical impacts of AI tools you use
- **Support accountability:** Demand transparency from AI companies about their energy use and labor practices
- **Advocate for regulation:** Push for policies requiring energy efficiency standards and ethical guidelines
- **Share knowledge:** Educate others about AI's environmental and social costs
- **Reduce unnecessary usage:** Be intentional about when AI assistance is truly valuable
- **Support alternatives:** Contribute to and use open-source, community-driven AI projects

### What Requires Systemic Change?

- **Energy infrastructure:** Transition data centers to 100% renewable energy requires grid-level infrastructure investment
- **Regulation:** International standards for AI energy efficiency and labor practices
- **Market incentives:** Economic pressure to prioritize sustainability over profit margins
- **Education reform:** Training and supporting data annotators with fair wages and working conditions
- **Research funding:** Governments and institutions need to invest in efficient AI development
- **Corporate accountability:** Mandatory reporting of environmental and ethical metrics

### Who Is Responsible?

**Multi-stakeholder responsibility:**
- **Tech companies:** Develop efficient models, ensure ethical labor practices, provide transparency
- **Governments:** Establish regulations, incentivize sustainable practices, support workers
- **Researchers:** Prioritize efficiency and ethics in AI development, publish transparent findings
- **Consumers/Users:** Make informed choices, demand accountability, support alternatives
- **Civil society:** Hold institutions accountable, advocate for workers and communities affected by AI

No single actor can solve this alone. Systemic change requires coordination across all stakeholders, with corporations and governments bearing the greatest responsibility due to their power and resources to effect large-scale change.
